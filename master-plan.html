01 — The Problem

We have been storing a problem,
not solving it.

Nuclear energy is having a global renaissance. Governments racing toward net-zero are turning back to it as the only proven source of low-carbon baseload power. In Canada, Ontario Power Generation is extending the life of the Pickering Nuclear Generating Station into the 2030s and refurbishing Darlington to run until 2055.

This is mostly good news. But it comes with a legacy problem no one has solved: the waste.

95%

24K yrs

The global strategy for managing this waste can be summarized in a single word: storage. Concrete and steel dry casks at reactor sites. Deep geological repositories hundreds of metres underground. These approaches do not neutralize the waste — they move it from one place to another, indefinitely, at perpetual cost and perpetual risk.

The Canadian Dimension

The Pickering Nuclear Generating Station stores dry nuclear waste on the shore of Lake Ontario — the drinking water source for 9 million people in the Greater Toronto Hamilton Area. The federal government distributes potassium iodide pills to every home within 10 km of the station. That radius includes the City of Toronto. Ontario Power Generation is currently extending Pickering's operational life, meaning waste volumes here will increase, not decrease. A significant radiation release could affect food and water supplies within a 50 km radius of one of Canada's most densely populated corridors. This is not a distant hypothetical — it is a managed risk that sits, literally, on the shore of our drinking water.

The fundamental reason no one has chemically neutralized nuclear waste is that radioactive decay is a nuclear phenomenon, not a chemical one. You cannot change a decay rate through chemistry. What you can do, however, is something more practical and more achievable: physically remove radioactive ions from the environment — capturing them, concentrating them, and converting them into stable, immobile, collectible solid forms. This is bioremediation. The barrier has never been the concept. It has been the tools required to design an organism capable of doing it precisely and at scale for the most dangerous elements on Earth. Those tools now exist.

02 — Our Mission

Design the organism
nature never built.

"A quantum computer designs a molecular key. Prime editing builds that key into a living organism. The organism uses the key to lock away nuclear waste permanently — converting it from dangerous liquid into stable, inert crystals."

Certain bacteria already interact with radioactive elements. Geobacter sulfurreducens pulls dissolved uranium out of groundwater. Deinococcus radiodurans — nicknamed "Conan the Bacterium" — survives up to 1.5 million rads of radiation through an extraordinary DNA repair system. These organisms evolved these capabilities incidentally, through billions of years of pressure. Their binding proteins were never optimized. Their range is narrow. Their efficiency is limited.

We propose to build the organism nature never had time to build — one designed from first principles at the atomic level, using computational tools that did not exist five years ago.

03 — The Solution

Search — Simulate — Solve.

Three technologies, integrated into a single pipeline. Each one is advancing rapidly in isolation. The moonshot is combining them at the right moment.

01 — Quantum Simulation

Map the atom exactly as it is.

Actinide atoms — Uranium, Plutonium, Neptunium — have up to 94 electrons, all influencing each other simultaneously. Classical computers cannot solve this. The number of calculations required exceeds what any computer could complete before the end of the universe.

A quantum processor running the Variational Quantum Eigensolver (VQE) algorithm simulates this electron structure natively — it "thinks" in the same quantum language as the atom itself. The output is a precise map of every site on the atom's surface where a protein could form a stable chemical bond. This map is the upstream input to everything that follows. Without it, protein design for actinides is guesswork. With it, you design from first principles.

02 — Protein Search

Find the optimal molecular key.

A protein that binds to Plutonium is a specific sequence of amino acids that folds into a shape precisely matching that atom's electron structure. For a protein of length n, there are 20n possible sequences — a combinatorially vast search space no classical algorithm can efficiently navigate.

A hybrid quantum-classical pipeline — using QAOA and ProteinMPNN — searches this space for the sequence producing the lowest-energy, most stable bond with the target binding sites. The result is a ranked list of candidate protein sequences: the molecular key, optimized computationally before a single experiment begins.

03 — Prime Editing

Build the key into a living organism.

Prime editing, developed at the Broad Institute in 2019, acts like a molecular find-and-replace. Unlike CRISPR — which cuts both DNA strands and relies on imprecise cellular repair — prime editing locates a specific sequence, removes it, and writes new DNA in its place with high precision and minimal off-target effects.

We use it to rewrite the DNA of Deinococcus radiodurans, inserting the quantum-designed protein gene and simultaneously enhancing the organism's natural radiation resistance. Precision here is not optional. A single off-target edit in an essential gene kills the organism entirely.

04 — Biomineralization

Convert liquid waste to solid crystals.

Biomineralization is the process by which organisms produce solid minerals — shellfish build calcium carbonate shells; bone cells produce hydroxyapatite. Engineered D. radiodurans will perform this same process for radioactive ions.

Deployed into nuclear waste, the bacteria survive the radiation environment, produce the quantum-designed metal-binding protein, capture radioactive ions from solution, and trigger mineralization — incorporating those ions into stable crystal structures that precipitate out of solution. The result: mobile, dangerous liquid radioactive waste converted into compact, immobile, collectible solid crystals. Not eliminated — transformed. Dramatically safer to store, in far smaller volumes, with negligible leaching risk.

04 — The Pipeline

From electron structure
to engineered crystal.

01

Quantum simulation of target actinide

A quantum processor running VQE simulates the electron cloud of the target radioactive atom — Uranium, Plutonium, or Neptunium. Output: a precise binding site map identifying coordinates, electron density values, and bond type at each interaction site. This map applies to every atom of that element, everywhere. Run once per element.

02

Quantum protein sequence search

The binding site map is fed into a hybrid quantum-classical search pipeline. Classical AI tools generate candidate protein sequences; the quantum algorithm evaluates binding energy for each candidate. Output: a ranked list of top protein sequences with predicted binding affinities and confidence scores.

03

Computational validation

Top candidates are validated using AlphaFold2 to confirm correct 3D folding inside a living cell. A protein that is optimal in simulation must also fold and function in a biological environment — this step is the bridge between computation and wet lab work.

04

Prime editing of D. radiodurans

Validated protein sequences are prime-edited into the D. radiodurans genome. Simultaneously, DNA repair genes (PprI) and antioxidant pathways (katA) are enhanced to bolster the organism's already-remarkable radiation resistance. Each actinide target produces a distinct engineered strain.

05

Biomineralization validation and deployment

Engineered strains are validated under controlled conditions — confirming ion capture, crystal formation, and crystal characterization via XRD. Strains are deployed sequentially through nuclear waste: Uranium first, then Plutonium, Neptunium, and Americium. Output: liquid radioactive waste converted to compact, collectible crystals.

05 — Why This Becomes Possible Now

Three conditions met
simultaneously for the first time.

This project is not speculative science fiction. It is the convergence of three distinct technological trajectories, each independently advancing, arriving at the right threshold at the same moment.

Condition 01

Quantum hardware reached utility scale in 2024.

IBM's 1,121-qubit Condor processor and Google's Willow chip represent the first generation of quantum hardware capable of running meaningful molecular simulations. Until 2024, processors were too noisy and limited. Full fault-tolerant quantum computing — the level required for precise actinide-scale VQE — is projected within 3–5 years. This capability literally did not exist before.

Condition 02

Prime editing was only published in 2019.

The precision genetic engineering tool required to make targeted, multi-gene edits in bacterial DNA without off-target effects simply did not exist before. Its application to bacterial systems is an active area of research today. The tool that makes this project's biological stage possible is, at most, six years old.

Condition 03

No one has combined these three fields — ever.

Quantum chemistry, synthetic biology, and nuclear waste remediation have developed in entirely separate scientific communities. Nuclear physicists do not speak to synthetic biologists. Synthetic biologists do not speak to environmental engineers working on nuclear sites. The insight that these three fields could form an integrated pipeline — where the output of one is the input to the next — is itself novel. This is not a project that was attempted and failed. It is a project that was never attempted because the component technologies did not exist simultaneously until now.

07 — Key Risks

We know what we don't know yet.

The risks here are real and specific. We do not minimize them. We have plans for each of them.

Regulatory complexity

Deploying genetically engineered organisms into nuclear waste sites requires simultaneous approval from nuclear regulators (CNSC), environmental agencies, and biosafety bodies. No GMO has ever been approved for deployment in a nuclear context. This pathway could take a decade or more — making it the single highest-risk long-term blocker.

Mitigation

Phase 1 goals focus entirely on contained lab results. Regulatory engagement begins in parallel with technical development, not after. Canada's CNSC has demonstrated progressive engagement with novel nuclear technologies.

Simulation-to-biology gap

A protein optimal in quantum simulation may not fold correctly inside a living cell, or may be degraded by cellular proteases, or may not be expressed at sufficient levels to function at scale. Computational prediction and biological reality are not identical.

Mitigation

Iterative validation at every handoff. AlphaFold2 confirms folding before synthesis. Wet lab binding tests confirm function before any organism engineering begins. The pipeline is explicitly designed to be iterative, not one-directional.

Multi-stressor survival

Nuclear waste environments combine radiation damage, chemical toxicity, and extreme pH or ionic conditions simultaneously. These stressors interact nonlinearly. Even D. radiodurans, with its remarkable natural resilience, has not been validated under the full combination present in high-level waste.

Mitigation

Modular strain approach: engineer one organism per actinide target. Incrementally test survival under increasingly representative conditions. Build in explicit failure checkpoints before progressing to higher-complexity environments.

Quantum hardware timeline

Fault-tolerant quantum computing — the level required for full actinide-scale VQE — is projected within 3–5 years. That projection could slip. Hardware timelines in quantum computing have historically been optimistic.

Mitigation

The pipeline is built now on small proxy atoms, so the biology team does not wait for quantum hardware. When hardware arrives, only the input changes — the pipeline is already built and validated. Classical approximations provide interim utility.

08 — The 10x Difference

Not incrementally better.
Categorically different.

Dimension Current global approach This approach
Mechanism Store waste, wait 10,000+ years for natural decay Actively transform waste into a different, safer material state
Design basis Natural bacteria discovered incidentally with limited capability Proteins and organisms designed from first principles at atomic level
Precision Trial-and-error biology, decades of iteration Quantum-computed optimal configuration before any experiment begins
Output form Mobile liquid waste requiring perpetual containment Immobile solid crystals — compact, collectible, negligible leaching risk
Cost trajectory Perpetual liability — U.S. DOE alone faces $44.5B in unresolved waste One-time engineered solution, redeployable as waste volumes grow
Underlying logic Treat the symptom — manage the hazard indefinitely Address the physical form — eliminate mobility and leaching risk at source

To be precise about what this achieves: biomineralization does not eliminate radioactivity. The crystals produced are still radioactive. What changes is their physical form — from mobile, soluble, high-surface-area liquid waste into dense, insoluble, low-surface-area solid crystals. This transformation dramatically reduces the probability of environmental contamination, reduces storage volume, and eliminates the leaching risk that makes liquid high-level waste so dangerous. That is the 10x: not a claim about destroying radioactivity, but a claim about fundamentally changing its containability.

09 — Why Canada, Why Now

The urgency is local.
The solution is global.

Canada is one of the world's leading nuclear nations, operating 19 CANDU reactors in Ontario. Two stations — Pickering and Darlington — both sit on the shores of Lake Ontario, the drinking water source for 9 million people, and both are being extended or refurbished to operate well into the 2050s. Waste volumes will increase. The risk, as currently managed, will grow.

This makes Canada the right place to begin. The urgency is immediate and local. The regulatory environment at the Canadian Nuclear Safety Commission is progressive and engaged with novel approaches. The customer — Ontario Power Generation — is identifiable, well-funded, and operates under increasing public scrutiny over the Pickering shoreline situation. And Canada's research infrastructure — with institutions like the University of Toronto, TRIUMF, and Canadian National Laboratories — provides the academic partnership substrate to execute this work.

But the problem is not Canadian. 390,000 metric tonnes of waste exist globally, growing as nuclear energy expands in response to climate commitments. The solution developed in Ontario becomes deployable at every nuclear site on Earth.

We face real technical risk, genuine regulatory complexity, and hardware timelines that could slip. We acknowledge each of these precisely, not optimistically. What we do not acknowledge is the suggestion that the current approach — perpetual storage, perpetual liability, perpetual risk — is acceptable.

The tools to build something better now exist. The urgency is immediate. The potential customer is a train ride from where we're building this.

It's time to build.
